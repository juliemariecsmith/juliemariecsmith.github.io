Instructor Presence







In a traditional classroom, there are natural avenues for building a relationship between students and the instructor, including informal chats right before and after class. Online, things are a bit trickier, and yet a strong relationship between the instructor and the students is just as important.

Critical inquiry in a text-based environment: Computer conferencing in higher education

The Community of Inquiry framework positions an online educational experience as the center of a Venn Diagram consisting of three circles: cognitive presence, social presence, and instructor presence. While all three are important, Garrison et al. note that it is instructor's presence which is the "binding element."

Researching the community of inquiry framework: Review, issues, and future directions

There is extensive evidence that instructor presence is a critical in online courses -- it is even more important than the relationships between students.

Instructor presence effect: Liking does not always lead to learning

Best practices for instructor presence are not always intuitive: for example, including a visual of the instructor in a video increased student distraction and decreased student learning.

Announce that you will open the virtual classroom ten minutes before class; during that time, chat with students and be open to their questions.

Have virtual office hours and specifically invite students to attend "just to say hi."

Host a virtual "coffee hour;" invite students to pop in for a chat.

Schedule one-on-one meetings with students throughout the semester; focus more on relationship-building than on specific learning goals.
