Personal Statement

After a brief stint teaching, I have spent most of my adult life homeschooling my kids. I enjoyed those years very much because they allowed me to teach in an environment with virtually no restrictions. This meant that we could mummify chickens as part of our study of ancient Egypt, jump on the couch as we memorized poems, spend countless hours reaching together, and enjoy long afternoons at the park.

As my boys grew, I realized that I needed a plan for the next stage of my life. I decided to update my familiarity with educational research by earning a graduate degree in curriculum and instruction. I also decided to take on part-time work to finance that project, and I was pleased to work on a variety of curricular projects. I initially balked when a client asked me to work on computing curriculum since I knew nothing about it. But they pressed, I eventually agreed, and the rest, as they say, is history.

I wanted to continue my education, so I selected the doctoral program in Learning Technologies at UNT since it had a strong focus on computing. I was very pleased to begin working with Dr. Norris, whose expertise in computer science has benefited me greatly. I also appreciated delving into the philosophical aspects of the field with Dr. Warren and sharpening my data analysis skills with Dr. Lee. Throughout the entire program, Dr. Foshay has been an excellent sounding board.

I have also benefitted greatly from associations outside of the program. I have worked on two NSF-funded projects through SageFox Consulting, which specializes in STEM-related educational research. Through this work, I was able to network with specialists in computer science education as well as hone my research and project management skills. I have recently begun to work with the Teaching Systems Lab at MIT, where I have been deeply impressed by the various skill sets of my colleagues as we work to expand access to and equity within computer science education. Similarly, as a fellow in the Cultural Competence in Computing Program, I am currently expanding my knowledge in this important aspect of education.

The silver lining to being a graduate student during a pandemic is that online conferences have made it easier to participate with a minimum commitment of time and money, especially given the international nature of computer science education research. I feel that I have learned a lot from preparing for and presenting at over a dozen conferences. I have also benefitted from being a teaching assistant during every semester of my studies; in addition to working directly with students, this position has shaped my understanding of best practices for online learning.

I am pleased with the fact that my work in computer science education research has not been overly narrow. I have performed qualitative research on historical interviews with female computer science pioneers as well as a content analysis on instructional materials; I have performed quantitative analysis on interviews with computer science professional development providers as well as data sets related to the Advanced Placement exams. I have developed tools for improving computing education as well as a tutorial for best practices for multimedia instructional materials.

Scholars who have deeply influenced my work include Amy J. Ko, Richard Mayer, David James, Mark Guzdial, Justin Reich, and Nicki Washington. While I can't reasonably aspire to match their accomplishments, I do aspire to match their dedication to improving learning, especially computer science education, with a focus on equity.

My professional interest is, put simply, to improve computer science education for all students. I don't yet know whether my best fit will be found in higher education, a research lab, a non-profit, or industry. Regardless, I'd like to focus on ways to incorporate the learning sciences into improving computing education. It is a truism in this field that learning to program is hard; I'd like to work to transform it into what one computing education pioneer, Seymour Papert, called 'hard fun.'

JULIE M. SMITH
